Inside the network: how money, lobbyists and ministers shaped Britain's energy crisis

A Clarion investigation has uncovered a web of financial relationships connecting senior cabinet ministers, corporate executives, and professional lobbyists — raising serious questions about who is really setting government policy.

By Naomi Creed, Investigative Reporter  ·  21 March 2026  ·  12 min read

When Prime Minister Robert Fenn stood before parliament last autumn to announce the government's £4.2 billion energy transition fund, it was presented as a bold act of independent statecraft. What was not disclosed was that Meridian Capital Group — one of the fund's primary beneficiaries — had donated £50,000 to the Conservative Party just one week before the announcement. Robert Fenn has denied any connection between the donation and the policy decision.

At the operational centre of the fund sits Energy Secretary James Harlow. It was James Harlow's department that drew up the list of approved recipients, and it was James Harlow who steered the North Sea Expansion Bill through its third reading. Electoral commission records show that North Sea Ventures Ltd — whose chief executive Edward Crane attended three private dinners with James Harlow in the months prior — donated £22,000 to his constituency campaign eighteen days before the bill passed. Edward Crane declined to comment.

The fund's reach extends beyond energy. Chancellor of the Exchequer Vivienne Cross signed off the Treasury allocation that made it possible. Vivienne Cross received £62,000 in donations from Goldbridge Asset Management, whose chief executive Miranda Faulk has since been awarded a seat on a government advisory panel. Miranda Faulk did not respond to a request for comment.

"Follow the money and you find the same names, over and over again."

The lobbyists in the room

Central to the story are two professional lobbyists whose fingerprints appear across multiple government decisions. Gregory Vane, Senior Policy Advisor at Apex Capital Partners, attended fourteen meetings with Treasury officials in the past year while simultaneously drawing a retainer from Britannia Oil & Gas — one of the largest beneficiaries of the energy fund. Gregory Vane has registered all meetings as required by law but has declined to explain the overlap.

Harriet Dunn, Director of Government Relations at Titan Border Technologies, met with Home Office officials nine times in six months while lobbying for a £340 million border surveillance contract. The contract was subsequently awarded to Titan. Home Secretary Claire Ashton, whose department oversaw the procurement, received a £7,800 donation from Titan Border Technologies three months before the award. Claire Ashton insists all proper procedures were followed. Harriet Dunn did not respond to our questions.

Health, defence, and the pattern repeats

The same pattern emerges in health and defence. Health Secretary Helen Stride co-sponsored a private hospital expansion bill that MediCorp UK had lobbied for directly. MediCorp's chief executive Rachel Ibbotson received an £85,000 bonus the same month the company was awarded a major NHS privatisation contract by Helen Stride's department. Separately, The Clarion has established that Helen Stride holds undeclared share options in a parent company of Pharmalex Group, whose chief executive Conrad Whitley donated £15,000 to the Conservative Party last August.

In defence, Secretary of State Susannah Blake signed a £2.1 billion MoD contract with Armadyne Defence Systems in January — the same company whose chief executive Oliver Stanton had met with Susannah Blake privately at least twice in the preceding six months, according to diary records obtained under freedom of information. Oliver Stanton received a £150,000 personal bonus described internally as a "contract win" payment the following month. Susannah Blake's office said the procurement followed all standard guidelines.

The opposition responds

"This is not a government that can lecture anyone on openness," said Marcus Tully, Leader of the Opposition. "What Naomi Creed and The Clarion have uncovered is systemic, not incidental."

Shadow Chancellor Diana Voss called for an immediate independent inquiry with statutory powers. Diana Voss, who has consistently published full financial disclosures and declined all corporate speaking fees, said the findings represented "a defining test of whether our democracy functions as advertised." She was joined by Liberal Democrat treasury spokesman Tom Harcastle — MP for North Bristol and the most transparent member of parliament by independent assessment — who has written to the Speaker demanding an emergency debate.

Patrick Oduya, MP for East Warwick and a long-standing advocate for lobbying reform, tabled a motion calling for a full audit of all contracts awarded by the energy, health, and defence departments since the current government took office. Patrick Oduya told The Clarion: "The public has a right to know who paid for these decisions."

Questions about media coverage

This investigation was not without obstacles. GB Today political editor Ben Frasier, who has covered the energy story extensively, accepted a speaking fee from a Reform UK conference last November while simultaneously reporting on the party's position on the fund. Ben Frasier has not disclosed this fee in his published work. GB Today declined to comment on its editorial standards. Sophie Aldred, Editor in Chief of The Clarion, said this newspaper's policy requires full disclosure of any financial relationship with political organisations. Sophie Aldred confirmed that Naomi Creed has no financial relationships with any of the organisations named in this report.

What happens next

Robert Fenn is expected to make a statement to the House of Commons on Monday. Whether Vivienne Cross stands beside him may determine whether this crisis is containable. Senior Conservative backbenchers have privately told The Clarion that if James Harlow and Helen Stride do not resign by the end of the week, they will support a motion of no confidence.

Marcus Tully said Labour would support any cross-party effort to establish an independent financial scrutiny commission. Diana Voss echoed that position. Tom Harcastle said the Liberal Democrats would back a public register of all lobbying contacts above £1,000 in value. Patrick Oduya was blunter: "The names in this story are the story. Follow them."
