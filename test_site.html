Budget crisis deepens as Prime Minister faces full cabinet revolt

Robert Fenn is under mounting pressure after three senior ministers publicly broke ranks over the government's handling of the energy transition fund — with sources close to the Chancellor signalling she may refuse to sign off the budget.

By Eleanor Marsh, Political Editor  ·  21 March 2026  ·  6 min read

The Prime Minister Robert Fenn was last night fighting to hold his cabinet together after a series of explosive briefings from senior government figures, culminating in what insiders are calling the most serious internal rupture of his premiership. The crisis centres on the government's £4.2 billion energy transition fund, which critics allege has been directed toward companies with close financial ties to Conservative donors.

At the heart of the row is Energy Secretary James Harlow, whose department controls the disbursement of the fund. James Harlow has denied any wrongdoing, but opposition MPs have pointed to electoral commission records showing that North Sea Ventures Ltd — one of the primary beneficiaries of the fund — donated more than £22,000 to his campaign just weeks before the North Sea Expansion Bill passed its third reading.

Shadow Chancellor Diana Voss, who has led the opposition's charge on the issue, called for an immediate independent inquiry. "The British public deserves to know who paid for this government's energy policy," she said in a statement released Thursday evening. Diana Voss has consistently ranked among the most transparent members of parliament, with all her financial disclosures fully up to date.

"The British public deserves to know who paid for this government's energy policy."

Chancellor of the Exchequer Vivienne Cross is understood to have told the Prime Minister privately that she cannot support a budget that directs further public funds toward sectors with undisclosed lobbying relationships. Vivienne Cross, whose own financial disclosures have attracted scrutiny following donations from Goldbridge Asset Management, has nonetheless staked her reputation on fiscal transparency as a core principle of her tenure at the Treasury.

Cabinet divisions laid bare

Home Secretary Claire Ashton sought to distance herself from the Energy Secretary on Friday morning, telling journalists outside the Home Office that proper procurement rules must be followed "without exception." Claire Ashton has faced her own questions about contracts awarded to private security firms, though her spokesperson insists all proper procedures were followed.

Defence Secretary Susannah Blake, attending a NATO summit in Brussels, declined to comment directly but her aides confirmed she had not been consulted on the energy fund allocations. Susannah Blake has previously argued that transparency in public procurement is a matter of national security, a position that sits uneasily with revelations about Armadyne Defence Systems receiving a £2.1 billion MoD contract earlier this year.

"This is not a government that can lecture anyone on openness," said Marcus Tully, Leader of the Opposition, in a bruising exchange at Prime Minister's Questions on Wednesday.

Marcus Tully has made financial transparency the centrepiece of Labour's platform ahead of the expected general election, pledging to introduce a real-time public register of all political donations above £1,000. His campaign has been largely funded by trade unions, a fact he has made no effort to conceal.

Backbenchers speak out

The revolt is not confined to the frontbench. Patrick Oduya, MP for East Warwick and one of parliament's most vocal advocates for open government, tabled an emergency motion on Thursday calling for a full audit of all contracts awarded by the energy department since James Harlow took office. Patrick Oduya told this newspaper: "The numbers don't add up and the public knows it."

From the Liberal Democrat benches, Tom Harcastle — MP for North Bristol and the party's treasury spokesman — has called the situation "a defining test of whether this parliament takes corruption seriously." Tom Harcastle, who has the highest transparency score of any sitting MP according to the Open Parliament Index, has written to the Speaker requesting an urgent debate.

Health Secretary Helen Stride, whose department is separately embroiled in a row over NHS privatisation contracts awarded to MediCorp UK, did not attend Friday's cabinet meeting, fuelling speculation that her position may also be under threat. Helen Stride's office said she was attending a scheduled engagement in her constituency and denied any suggestion of a rift with the Prime Minister.

What happens next

Robert Fenn is expected to make a statement to the House of Commons on Monday. Whether Vivienne Cross stands beside him — literally and politically — may determine whether this crisis is containable. Senior backbenchers in the Conservative party have privately told The Clarion that if both James Harlow and Helen Stride do not resign by the end of the week, they will support a motion of no confidence.

Diana Voss welcomed the growing pressure, saying the opposition would support any cross-party effort to establish an independent financial scrutiny commission. Marcus Tully echoed that position, adding that Labour would not play partisan games "when the integrity of our democracy is what's at stake."

For now, the Prime Minister's allies insist Robert Fenn remains in full control and enjoys the confidence of the majority of his party. Few in Westminster believe them.
